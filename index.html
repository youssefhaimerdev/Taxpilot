₿ Cryptocurrency Tax Guide

The IRS treats crypto as property, not currency. Every taxable event creates a gain or loss you must report — no exceptions.

⚠️ What Is a Taxable Crypto Event?

Selling crypto for USD
Capital gain/loss based on sale price minus cost basis.
Trading one crypto for another
BTC → ETH swap? That's a taxable sale of BTC at fair market value on the trade date.
Paying for goods/services with crypto
Treated as selling crypto at FMV on the day of purchase.
Staking rewards & mining income
Taxed as ordinary income at FMV when received. Then subject to capital gains if held and sold later.
Airdrops & hard fork tokens
Taxed as ordinary income at FMV on the date received.

✅ What Is NOT a Taxable Event?

Buying crypto with USD — no taxable event (just establishes cost basis)
Transferring crypto between your own wallets
Holding / HODLing crypto (no sale = no tax)
Donating crypto to a qualified charity (actually a deduction equal to FMV)

📊 Crypto Tax Rates (2025)

Held ≤ 1 year (short-term)Ordinary income rates (10–37%)
Held > 1 year (long-term)0%, 15%, or 20%
Staking / mining incomeOrdinary income rates
NFT salesCapital gains (collectible rules may apply)
DeFi yield / interestOrdinary income rates

📁 Recordkeeping Requirements

For every transaction you must track:

• Date of acquisition
• Amount acquired (in crypto and USD value)
• Date of sale or exchange
• Fair market value on date of sale (in USD)
• Cost basis (what you originally paid)
• Gain or loss (sale price minus cost basis)

💡 Tools: Use crypto tax software like Koinly, CoinTracker, or TokenTax (all have free tiers) to automatically import transactions from exchanges and calculate your gains/losses. Report on IRS Form 8949 and Schedule D.

🎯 Tax-Loss Harvesting: Crypto has no "wash sale" rule (unlike stocks). You can sell a losing position, claim the loss to offset gains, and immediately rebuy the same crypto. This strategy can save significant taxes in volatile years.

🤝 How to Work With Your Accountant

Even if you never meet an accountant, knowing this puts you ahead of 90% of taxpayers.

01

Bring the Right Documents

W-2s, 1099s, mortgage statements, investment statements, receipts for deductions, last year's return, Social Security numbers for dependents. Come prepared and save hourly fees.

02

Ask About Year-Round Planning

Tax season is too late to plan. Ask your accountant in October what you should do before December 31 — max retirement accounts, time income and expenses, harvest tax losses.

03

Understand Every Number They Sign

You are legally responsible for your return, not your accountant. Ask them to explain every major line item. A good CPA will happily explain — that's their job.

04

Verify Their Credentials

Use the IRS Directory of Preparers. Look for: CPA (licensed by state board), EA (Enrolled Agent, licensed by IRS), or Tax Attorney. Avoid anyone who promises "guaranteed refunds."

05

Know What to Say About Life Changes

Marriage, divorce, new baby, home purchase, job change, freelance income, inheritance, crypto — ALL of these change your tax picture. Never assume your accountant knows unless you tell them.

06

Questions to Ask Your CPA

"What's my most expensive deduction I'm not taking?" "Should I switch to S-Corp?" "Am I setting aside enough for quarterly taxes?" "Am I at risk for an audit?" These questions save thousands.

Who to Hire for What

CPA

Certified Public Accountant

Licensed by state boards. Best for complex personal returns, business taxes, audits, and financial planning. Highest credential for tax prep.

EA

Enrolled Agent

Licensed by the IRS itself. Unlimited practice rights before the IRS. Excellent for IRS problems, audits, appeals, and complex tax situations at lower cost than CPAs.

Tax Attorney

Tax Attorney

For serious legal disputes, criminal tax cases, offshore accounts, major estate planning, and situations where attorney-client privilege matters. Most expensive option.

The Complete Guide to US Taxes: Everything You Actually Need to Know

Millions of Americans — especially millennials and Gen Z — file taxes every year without fully understanding what they're paying, why they're paying it, or how to legally pay less. This guide covers everything: from your first W-2 to running a corporation, from talking to your accountant to understanding capital gains. Consider this your permanent tax reference.

What Is Federal Income Tax and How Does It Actually Work?

The United States uses a progressive tax system, meaning different portions of your income are taxed at different rates. In 2025, there are seven tax brackets ranging from 10% to 37%. The most important thing most people misunderstand: your entire income is NOT taxed at your "tax bracket" rate. Only the portion of income that falls in each bracket is taxed at that rate.

For example, if you're single and earn $60,000 in 2025, your first $11,925 is taxed at 10%, the next $36,550 is taxed at 12%, and only the remaining amount is taxed at 22%. Your marginal rate is 22%, but your effective rate — what you actually pay overall — is much lower, around 13-14%.

💡 Key insight: Most Americans in the 22% or 24% bracket have an effective rate of 10–16%. Understanding this difference is one of the most powerful concepts in tax literacy.

Standard Deduction vs. Itemized Deductions: Which Should You Take?

Before calculating your tax, you subtract either the standard deduction or your itemized deductions — whichever is larger. In 2025, the standard deduction is $15,000 for single filers and $30,000 for married couples filing jointly. These amounts increase every year with inflation.

You should itemize only if your deductible expenses (mortgage interest, state taxes, charity, medical) exceed the standard deduction. For most Americans, the standard deduction wins — especially after the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 nearly doubled it. High earners in expensive states, homeowners with large mortgages, and those with significant medical bills are the most likely candidates for itemizing.

W-2 Employee vs. 1099 Contractor: The Tax Difference That Changes Everything

As a W-2 employee, your employer withholds federal and state income tax from each paycheck and pays half of your FICA taxes (7.65% Social Security + Medicare). You receive your W-2 in January and file your return — often getting a refund because withholding is estimated.

As a 1099 independent contractor, no taxes are withheld. You pay the full 15.3% self-employment tax (both employee and employer share), plus income tax. This surprises many first-time freelancers who realize in April they owe a large lump sum. The solution: pay quarterly estimated taxes using IRS Form 1040-ES every quarter.

⚠️ The 1099 trap: If you're used to getting a W-2 refund and switch to freelancing, you might owe thousands in April instead. Set aside 25-30% of every freelance payment for taxes immediately.

Self-Employment Tax: The Tax Nobody Warns You About

Self-employment tax exists because employees and employers each pay 7.65% for Social Security and Medicare. When you're self-employed, you're both — so you pay the full 15.3%. In 2025, Social Security applies to the first $176,100 of income, while Medicare applies to all income (with an additional 0.9% surcharge above $200,000).

The good news: you can deduct 50% of your self-employment tax from your gross income before calculating income tax — a partial offset built into the tax code. You can also deduct 100% of your self-employed health insurance premiums and contribute to retirement accounts like a SEP-IRA (up to 25% of net income, max $69,000) or Solo 401(k) to dramatically reduce your taxable income.

LLC, S-Corp, and C-Corp: Choosing the Right Business Structure for Tax Purposes

The business entity you choose determines how your income is taxed, and the stakes are significant. A sole proprietorship or single-member LLC pays self-employment tax on 100% of profits. An S-Corporation only pays FICA on your salary — not on the "owner distribution" portion of your income — potentially saving $3,000 to $15,000 per year in taxes for profitable businesses.

A C-Corporation pays a flat 21% corporate tax rate, but then shareholders also pay personal income tax on dividends — the dreaded "double taxation." C-Corps are primarily chosen by venture-backed startups that need to issue preferred shares to investors. For most small businesses earning under $500K, an S-Corp or LLC structure is more tax-efficient.

The Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction — available to pass-through entities (sole props, LLCs, S-Corps, partnerships) — allows you to deduct up to 20% of qualified business income from your personal return. In 2025, this deduction saves pass-through business owners enormous amounts. It phases out for high-income "specified service" businesses like law and accounting.

Capital Gains Tax: What Every Investor and Property Owner Must Understand

When you sell an asset — stocks, real estate, cryptocurrency, a business — the profit is a capital gain. Assets held for one year or less are taxed as ordinary income (your regular bracket). Assets held for more than one year qualify for long-term capital gains rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your income — significantly lower than ordinary income rates.

In 2025, if your total taxable income is under $48,350 (single) or $96,700 (married), you pay zero federal capital gains tax on long-term gains. This is one of the most powerful tax-planning tools available: a low-income year to sell appreciated assets with no tax consequences.

For cryptocurrency, the IRS treats every transaction — including trading one crypto for another — as a taxable event. NFT sales, staking rewards, and airdrops are all taxable. Keep meticulous records of your cost basis for every crypto transaction.

For home sales, you can exclude up to $250,000 of gain ($500,000 for married couples) if you've lived in the home as your primary residence for at least 2 of the last 5 years. This is one of the most generous exclusions in the tax code.

Tax Deductions for Homeowners: Mortgage Interest, Property Tax, and the Home Office

Homeownership comes with significant tax advantages. You can deduct mortgage interest on up to $750,000 of acquisition debt (loans used to buy, build, or improve the home). For a $500,000 mortgage at 7%, that's up to $35,000 of interest in the first year — a massive itemized deduction.

State and local property taxes are deductible up to the $10,000 SALT cap when combined with state income taxes. If you work from home and are self-employed, the home office deduction allows you to deduct a portion of rent/mortgage, utilities, and internet based on the percentage of your home used exclusively for business.

Vehicle Tax Deductions: Standard Mileage vs. Actual Expense Method

Business use of your vehicle is deductible two ways. The standard mileage rate in 2025 is $0.70 per business mile — straightforward and requires only a mileage log. The actual expense method lets you deduct the business-use percentage of gas, insurance, repairs, depreciation, and registration fees — better for expensive vehicles with high business use.

For electric vehicles purchased in 2025, the Clean Vehicle Credit offers up to $7,500 for qualifying new EVs and $4,000 for used EVs, subject to income limits and vehicle price caps. The credit is non-refundable for federal purposes.

Retirement Accounts: The Best Legal Tax Shelters Available

Contributing to tax-advantaged retirement accounts is the single most impactful year-round tax strategy for most Americans. A Traditional 401(k) lets you contribute up to $23,500 pre-tax in 2025 ($31,000 if 50+), reducing your taxable income dollar-for-dollar. A Traditional IRA allows $7,000 in deductible contributions ($8,000 if 50+), subject to income limits if covered by a workplace plan.

A Roth IRA or Roth 401(k) takes after-tax contributions but grows tax-free — all qualified withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free. For young earners who expect to be in higher brackets later, Roth is often the better long-term choice. A Health Savings Account (HSA) offers a rare triple tax advantage: contributions are deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free.

Understanding Corporate Tax Planning for Established Businesses

The corporate tax rate of 21% applies to all C-Corporation profits. Corporations can reduce taxable income through legitimate deductions: employee salaries and benefits, office expenses, equipment depreciation, research and development costs, and business interest expenses. The R&D Tax Credit (Section 41) allows qualifying businesses to reduce taxes owed by 20% of qualifying research expenditures — a powerful incentive for tech and innovation-driven companies.

Bonus depreciation allows businesses to immediately expense a percentage of the cost of qualifying property. In 2025, the rate is 40% (down from 100% in prior years as it phases out under current law). Combined with Section 179 expensing (up to $1.16 million in 2025), businesses can significantly front-load depreciation deductions in the year of purchase.

Common Tax Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

The most common and costly tax mistakes include: failing to track business expenses and losing deductions, not paying quarterly estimated taxes and incurring penalties, mixing personal and business finances (always keep separate accounts), forgetting to report side income, taking the wrong deductions on home offices or vehicles, not maximizing retirement contributions, and missing the filing deadline without requesting an extension. Filing Form 4868 for an automatic 6-month extension costs nothing and avoids the steep failure-to-file penalty of 5% per month.

Tax Scams: Protect Yourself

The IRS will never call you to demand immediate payment. The IRS will never email you demanding action. The IRS will never threaten police arrest over the phone. If you receive such contact, hang up and report it. All legitimate IRS correspondence arrives by USPS mail. The IRS's primary communication tool is the letter — always verify any tax notice is real by calling the IRS directly at 1-800-829-1040 or looking up the notice number on IRS.gov.
